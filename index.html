My Philosophy

Modeling social topics allows us to move beyond superficial lenses and gain deeper understanding of the invisible qualities that affect lives. Using data science to model social behavior enables us to identify systemic inequalities, track information spread, and understand complex social and scientific questions—such as how urban environments affect mental health.

When we use data about people, we're not just handling information; we're handling power. This brings significant responsibilities: contextual integrity, privacy and dignity, and mitigating bias. Algorithms are susceptible to bias—if you train a model on biased historical data, you're not achieving "objectivity" but rather scaling that bias.

I'm bridging the gap between technical proficiency and ethical awareness. I want to understand not just how to build predictive models, but how to critique them. Through interdisciplinary learning in sociology, ethics, and political science, I'm becoming a better data scientist.

My focus is on social modeling and engineering—using data and ML to understand communities and find ways to improve people's lives. Whether addressing health issues or mapping socioeconomic challenges in cities like Charlotte, data science enables powerful positive change. My goal is to empower and uplift communities rather than simply observe them.
