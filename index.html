Reflections & Insights

Modeling Social Topics & Power (Jan 16, 2026)

Modeling social topics allows us to move beyond superficial lenses and get a deeper understanding on the invisible qualities that affect lives. Using data science to model social behavior allows us to identify systemic inequalities, track the spread of information (and misinformation), and understand a variety of social and scientific questions such as how urban environments affect mental health.

When we use data about people, we aren't just handling information; we are handling power. This brings a heavy set of responsibilities such as contextual integrity, privacy and dignity, and mitigating bias. Algorithms are susceptible to be trained on bias. If you train a model on biased historical data, you might not be "objective" but rather scaling that bias.

Through this course I am hoping to bridge the gap between technical proficiency and ethical awareness. I want to understand not just how to build a predictive model, but how to critique one. I’m eager to learn how interdisciplinary fields like sociology, ethics, and political science which all can make me a better data scientist. Data science enables us to make effective and powerful changes in the world to ultimately create a better world for generations.

Ethical Ambiguity in Modeling (Jan 28, 2026)

To me, ethical ambiguity in modeling is the grey area where technical success and social harm intertwine. For example, it shows up when we use proxy variables like "zip code" as a stand-in for creditworthiness that inadvertently integrates systemic inequality into an algorithm.

Stakeholder Engagement: We cannot and shouldn't model communities and things we don’t understand. By involving those impacted during the design phase, we go from observing data to understanding the people behind the numbers.

Model Interpretability: If we cannot explain why the model reached a specific conclusion, we cannot effectively check it for bias. Interpretability ensures decisions are based on logic rather than hidden prejudices.

Ongoing Monitoring: A model that is ethical at launch can become unethical as social conditions change. Continuous monitoring ensures that as the real world evolves, our models don't become tools for unintentional discrimination.
